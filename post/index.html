Introduction

 In general, the stock market is a crazy place to make money.  Stock prices move randomly on an exchange, making it a gamble for investors who want accrue income quickly. In an ideal world, people want to make riskless profit.  Unfortunately, this is not an ideal world, and often times arbitrage is tough to find.   For someone to be largely rewarded often requires them to take on large risk.  Notwithstanding, however, quantitative financial analysts have cleverly figured out ways to attract luck.  An investor who employs a strategy devised by a great quant will be able to hedge their bets to lessen their risk and still make sizeable gains. 

 

 

In the 1950s, an academic named Harry Markowitz who specialized in financial economics devised a way to apportion weights in a multi-asset portfolio that would maximize its returns and minimize its risk.  His ideas were published in The Journal of Finance, and they served as the zeitgeist for Modern Portfolio Theory and mean variance optimization.  Markowitz’ breakthrough was so revolutionary that he was awarded the Nobel Prize.  Billions of dollars have been gained through the strategic employment of mean-variance optimization

 

 

In the 1980s, two quants at Morgan Stanley, Gerry Bamberger and Nunzio Tartaglia, devised a trading strategy known as pairs trading.  It rested on the idea of directly hedging bets to make very risk-adjusted returns.  Tartaglia’s group reportedly made over $50 million profit for the firm in 1987.

Screening for Cointegrated Pairs

 

The first step in the project is to identify pairs of cointegrated stocks. The problem of finding strongly cointegrated pairs is computationally challenging for a large group of stocks. The number of potential pairs grows quadratically as a function of the number of stocks in the group (i.e. for a list of n stocks, we have (n)(n-1)/2 potential pairs). A naive approach would take a large basket of randomly selected stocks and scan for all cointegrated pairs in that basket. The core issue with this approach is that the likelihood of any two stocks being strongly cointegrated is relatively small -thus, not many strongly cointegrated pairs would be identified in a large number of cointegration tests. In order to improve the search, the search is refined to a basket of stocks that contain strongly related companies. This is achieved by using a basket of 100 tech stocks in the S&P 500.

After picking this basket, a cointegration test is performed on each of the 4,950 potential pairs. We use the augmented Engle-Granger two-step cointegration test, which works as follows:

●       The goal is to determine whether two time series, x_t and y_t, are strongly cointegrated. In other words, is there a linear combination of x_t and y_t such that this process is stationary (i.e. y_t = mu_t + beta_1*x_t where mu_t is stationary)

●       Step (1): Estimate beta using ordinary least squares and solve for the process mu_t = y_t - beta_1*x_t. Then use the Augmented Dickey Fuller test to check for stationarity of mu_t. Store the residuals from this OLS for step (2)

●       Step (2): Estimate an error correction model. This is done by regressing the differenced y_t on the differenced x_t and the residuals (with lag of 1) from step (1). Then test for cointegration by assessing the t-statistic for the regression coefficients.

            Using the two-step cointegration test and setting a p-value threshold of five percent, we finished this portion of the project with a basket of 75 pairs that are cointegrated with at least 95% confidence.

 

Implementing a Pairs Trading Strategy

 

        The next step in our project is to implement a pairs trading strategy for each of the 75 cointegrated pairs of stocks. We will present the basics of the strategy and then demonstrate how to further implement a more complex model. A “bare-bones” pairs trading strategy works as follows:

●       Find a cointegrated pair of stocks. The cointegration relationship allows us to give relative value between the pair of stocks.

○       the pair follows the model: y_t = mu_t + beta_1*x_t where mu_t is stationary

●       If mu_t is sufficiently larger than it’s mean then x_t is undervalued relative to y_t

○       The strategy will long x_t, short y_t

●        If mu_t is sufficiently smaller than it’s mean then x_t is overvalued relative to y_t

○        The strategy will long y_t, short y_t

 

            The above strategy is much too basic. A strong cointegration relationship demonstrates that the ratio of the stock prices is a stationary process. However, this does not mean that there will be many opportunities to make profits. Consider an implementation of this basic strategy on Oracle and Microsoft stocks, one of the 75 pairs identified via the 95% confidence cointegration test.  The plot below[3]  shows the ratio of Oracle stock to Microsoft stock. Because the stocks are cointegrated, this ratio is a stationary process about the mean ratio of 0.279.

In the basic strategy, we decide to invest when the ratio is sufficiently far from it’s mean -in this case, we choose a delta of .01. When the process crosses the green line, the strategy goes long Oracle and short Microsoft. When the process crosses the red line, the strategy goes short Oracle and long Microsoft. Although this pair was found to be very strongly cointegrated, there are only a few moments in this 18-month period where there is an opportunity to make a trade. We will now demonstrate a more involved pairs trading strategy that will allow for more opportunities to make trades and profits.

 

            In order to create more profits, our team constructed a flexible and adaptive trading model which allows for more opportunities to make trades. We follow a similar strategy as presented above, however, instead of using a constant mean and delta to completely determine our trading signals we use non-constant terms. In particular, our new strategy does the following:

●       Compute the 5-day and 60-day rolling averages of the ratio process and the 60-day rolling standard deviation

●       Compute a “Z-score” as mu5-mu60/std60 which will be used to determine if the current ratio is significantly far from away from its average

●       When the Zscore is below -1, long

●       When the Zscore is above +1, short
